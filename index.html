About

I study AI as a sociotechnical system, working at the intersection of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), Computer-Supported Cooperative Work (CSCW), and Technology Policy. Using qualitative methods, I examine what happens when AI technologies encounter democratic institutions—investigating the friction, negotiation, resistance, and regulation that emerge.

I consider myself a "bilingual" researcher with an unconventional path: from engineering through qualitative research to government policy. I previously served as lead for the Chilean National AI Policy, coordinating dialogue among policymakers, industry, academia, and the public. I currently advise international organizations including UNESCO and the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB).

My dissertation research investigates how AI technologies negotiate with institutional reality, using friction as a generative force to understand resistance, regulation, and participatory enhancement.

Committee: Cristobal Cheyre (advisor), Qian Yang, Malte Ziewitz
